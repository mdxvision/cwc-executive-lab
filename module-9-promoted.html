Get Promoted

Strategic career advancement for women of color

Let's Be Honest

Hard work alone doesn't get you promoted. If it did, you'd already be there. Promotion requires strategy, visibility, advocacy‚Äîand understanding how decisions actually get made.

The Myth

If I do excellent work, keep my head down, and exceed expectations, I'll be recognized and promoted.

This rarely works‚Äîespecially for WOC.

The Reality

Promotions go to people who are known, who have advocates, who make their case, and who are seen as ready for the next level.

Visibility + Advocacy + Readiness = Promotion

The Promotion Equation

What Actually Drives Promotion Decisions

Performance + Visibility + Advocacy = Promotion

Performance (Table Stakes)

Excellent work is necessary but not sufficient. Without it, nothing else matters‚Äîbut with it alone, you'll stay stuck.

Visibility (The Multiplier)

Decision-makers must know who you are and what you've accomplished. Your work must be seen by those who matter.

Advocacy (The Accelerator)

You need people with power speaking on your behalf in rooms you're not in. This is where promotions are won or lost.

Performance That Gets Noticed

Doing the right work, not just more work

Focus on High-Impact Work

Not all work is created equal. Promotions reward work that's visible, valued, and connected to business priorities‚Äînot the most hours or the most effort.

High-Impact Work

  • Tied to company strategic priorities
  • Visible to senior leadership
  • Creates measurable results
  • Involves cross-functional exposure
  • Demonstrates leadership capability

Low-Visibility Work

  • "Office housework" (note-taking, planning parties)
  • Maintenance work that keeps things running
  • Helping others without credit
  • Projects no one asked for
  • Work your boss takes credit for

The Question to Ask

"Will doing this well advance my career, or just add to my workload?"

Document Your Impact

If you don't track your wins, no one will. Keep a running record of your accomplishments using the STAR framework:

S

Situation

The context and challenge

T

Task

Your responsibility

A

Action

What you specifically did

R

Result

Measurable outcome

Weekly Practice

Every Friday, spend 10 minutes adding to your accomplishments log. Include: project name, your specific contribution, quantifiable results, who saw it. This becomes your promotion evidence.

Securing Advocacy

Getting people to speak for you

You Need Sponsors, Not Just Mentors

Mentors

Talk TO you. Give advice. Help you think through decisions.

Valuable, but they don't get you promoted.

Sponsors

Talk ABOUT you. Advocate in rooms you're not in. Use their power for your advancement.

These are the people who get you promoted.

Research shows WOC are over-mentored and under-sponsored. We get plenty of advice but not enough advocacy. Focus your energy on earning sponsors.

How to Earn a Sponsor

Sponsorship is earned, not asked for. Sponsors invest in people who make them look good:

1

Deliver Outstanding Results

Sponsors stake their reputation on you. Give them undeniable evidence of your excellence. Exceed expectations visibly.

2

Support Their Priorities

Volunteer for their initiatives. Make their job easier. Show you understand what they care about and can deliver on it.

3

Be Visible to Them

They can't sponsor what they don't know. Present in meetings they attend. Update them on wins. Build genuine relationship.

4

Be Clear About Your Goals

Once trust is built, share your aspirations. "I'm working toward [role]. I'd value your guidance on what I need to get there."

Having the Promotion Conversation

Asking for what you've earned

Timing Your Ask

Good Times to Ask

  • After a major successful project
  • During performance review cycles
  • When you've been doing the job already
  • When a position opens up
  • When your boss has budget authority

Not Ideal Times

  • During company layoffs or freezes
  • When your boss is under pressure
  • Before you've built your case
  • Right after a setback or mistake
  • When you haven't signaled interest before

The Pre-Conversation

Plant the seed early. "I want to discuss my path to [role]. Can we schedule time to talk about what I need to get there?"

Making Your Case

1

Lead with Impact

"Over the past [time], I've [specific accomplishments with measurable results]. I've consistently exceeded expectations in [areas]."

2

Show You're Already Doing the Job

"I've been operating at the [next level] by [examples]. I'm already doing [responsibilities] of that role."

3

Make the Ask Clear

"I'm ready for the [specific role/level] and believe I've demonstrated the impact to support this. I'd like to discuss making this official."

4

Address Potential Objections

If there are gaps, acknowledge them: "I know [area] is a growth area. Here's how I'm addressing it..."

When the Answer Is "Not Yet"

A "no" isn't the end‚Äîit's information. Your job is to understand what's needed and create accountability for getting there.

Questions to Ask

  • "What specifically do I need to demonstrate?"
  • "What timeline are we looking at?"
  • "What would change your answer?"
  • "Who else needs to be on board?"
  • "How can I get more visibility for my work?"

Follow-Up Actions

  • Email summary of the conversation
  • Create a 90-day development plan
  • Schedule regular check-ins on progress
  • Document all achievements
  • Revisit the conversation on agreed timeline

Know When to Look Elsewhere

"Sometimes the only way to get promoted is to get promoted out."
‚Äî Career Truth

Signs It's Time to Look

  • Vague feedback with no clear path
  • Goal posts keep moving
  • Others promoted ahead of you repeatedly
  • No sponsor willing to advocate
  • Company culture doesn't support your growth

The External Path

External opportunities often come with:

  • Significant title advancement
  • 15-20%+ salary increase
  • Fresh start without baggage
  • Expanded scope and challenge
  • Leverage for internal negotiation

Navigating as a WOC

The specific challenges we face

The Extra Work We Do

WOC often face additional barriers: we're held to higher standards, receive less sponsorship, and must navigate bias while still delivering excellent work.

Common Barriers

  • Being "too aggressive" or "not assertive enough"
  • Less access to informal networks
  • Work attributed to others or teams
  • Judged on potential less than peers
  • Expected to prove more to earn less

Strategic Responses

  • Document everything meticulously
  • Build multiple sponsors (don't rely on one)
  • Claim credit explicitly and often
  • Find allies who will amplify you
  • Know your market value externally
