Module 5
The Challenge

Why It's Harder for WOC
to Be Heard

The barriers to being heard aren't about your communication skills. They're about systems that weren't designed for your voice.

Cultural Programming

Raised to defer, accommodate, and not "make waves." Speaking up can feel like defiance.

Double Bind

Speak confidently = aggressive. Speak softly = not leadership material. You can't win.

Invisible Labor

Often asked to listen, support, and translate—rarely given the floor to lead.

Framework

The Voice Amplification Model

Three dimensions of speaking up effectively: what you say, how you say it, and when you say it.

Message Clarity

Lead with your point. Be concise. Remove qualifiers. Say what you mean with confidence.

Delivery Power

Use vocal variety, strategic pauses, and body language that commands attention.

Strategic Timing

Know when to speak first, when to wait, and how to insert yourself into the conversation.

Module 5
Message Clarity

Say It Clean, Say It Clear

Strip away the verbal hedges and qualifiers that undermine your message.

Remove These

  • "I just think maybe..."
  • "This might be a dumb idea, but..."
  • "I'm not sure if this is right..."
  • "Does that make sense?"
  • "Sorry, but..."

Replace With

  • "My recommendation is..."
  • "Here's my perspective..."
  • "Based on my analysis..."
  • "What questions do you have?"
  • (Just make your point)
Module 5
Delivery Power

How You Say It Matters

Your delivery can either amplify or undermine your message. Master these elements.

The Power of the Pause

Pause before key points. Pause after. Let silence do the work. Don't rush to fill space.

End Strong

Statements go down in pitch, not up. "We should do X." not "We should do X?"

Volume and Pace

Slow down for emphasis. Speak from your diaphragm. Take up auditory space.

Physical Presence

Sit forward. Use gestures. Make eye contact. Your body reinforces your words.

Reflection

You don't need permission to speak. You need practice speaking without it.

Module 5
Strategic Timing

When to Speak Up

Timing is strategy. Know when your voice will have the most impact.

1

Speak in the First 5 Minutes

Early contributions establish you as a participant, not an observer. Get your voice in the room.

2

Build on Your Own Ideas

If you raised a point earlier, reference it. "As I mentioned before..." reclaims ownership.

3

Summarize and Redirect

Taking the summary role gives you power: "Let me synthesize what I'm hearing..."

Module 5
Handling Interruptions

When You're Talked Over

Interruptions are common for WOC. Here's how to handle them with grace and power.

Keep Going

"I'd like to finish my thought..." Continue speaking at the same pace and volume. Don't yield.

Name It Neutrally

"I wasn't quite done." "I'd like to complete my point." Simple, direct, not apologetic.

Use an Ally

Prep a trusted colleague to say "I think [your name] wasn't finished..." Amplification works.

Circle Back

"Before we move on, I want to return to what I was saying..." Reclaim your idea.

Module 5
Idea Reclamation

When Someone Takes Your Idea

The "bro-propriation" of ideas is real. Here's how to reclaim credit without seeming petty.

In the Moment

  • "Thanks for championing my idea."
  • "I'm glad you're supporting what I proposed."
  • "Yes, that builds on my earlier point."
  • "Exactly what I was saying earlier."

Preventively

  • Document your ideas in writing first
  • Send a pre-meeting email with your thoughts
  • Follow up with written summaries
  • Build allies who will credit you

Module 5
Difficult Conversations

Speaking Up When It's Hard

The most important moments to use your voice are often the most uncomfortable.

When to Speak Up

  • When you disagree with direction
  • When you see a risk others don't
  • When you need resources or support
  • When you witness something wrong
  • When you want an opportunity

How to Frame It

  • Lead with the goal, not the problem
  • Use "I" statements about your perspective
  • Ask permission to share a different view
  • Offer solutions, not just concerns
  • Express directly what you need
Reflection

The room needs your voice. Don't deprive it—or yourself—of your contribution.
