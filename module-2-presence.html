Module 2
The Challenge

The Double Bind Is Real

WOC leaders face contradictory expectations that white male leaders rarely encounter. You're told to be confident—but not "aggressive." Warm—but not "soft." Visible—but not "attention-seeking."

"Too Much"

Too assertive, too loud, too ambitious, too visible, too direct, too passionate.

"Not Enough"

Not strategic enough, not visible enough, not confident enough, not polished enough.

The Truth

These contradictions aren't about you. They're about others' discomfort with your power.

Framework

The Three Pillars of
Executive Presence

Research identifies three core dimensions. Mastering all three creates the "complete package" that opens doors.

G

Gravitas

How you carry yourself. Your confidence, composure under pressure, and ability to command respect.

C

Communication

How you speak. Your ability to articulate ideas clearly, listen actively, and influence through words.

A

Appearance

How you show up visually. Your personal brand through clothing, grooming, and physical presence.

Gravitas
Pillar 1

Gravitas: Own Your Authority

Gravitas is the foundation of executive presence. It's not about being loud—it's about being grounded.

What Gravitas Looks Like

  • Composure under pressure
  • Decisive in uncertainty
  • Comfortable with silence
  • Takes up space without apology
  • Holds eye contact naturally

Building Your Gravitas

  • Pause before responding—don't rush
  • State opinions as statements, not questions
  • Sit at the table, not against the wall
  • Eliminate hedge words ("just," "I think maybe")
  • Practice the power of the pause
Communication
Pillar 2

Communication: Speak to Influence

It's not just what you say—it's how you say it, when you say it, and to whom.

High-Impact Communication

  • Lead with the headline
  • Use concrete, specific language
  • Vary pace and tone intentionally
  • Ask powerful questions
  • Listen to understand, not to respond

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Upspeak (ending statements as questions?)
  • Over-explaining or over-qualifying
  • Apologizing for having an opinion
  • Letting others take credit for your ideas
  • Staying silent when your voice matters
Appearance
Pillar 3

Appearance: Align the Visual

Appearance is the most visible—and often most policed—dimension for WOC. Your goal: intentional alignment, not conformity.

Questions to Ask

  • Does my visual presence match my role?
  • Am I dressing for where I am or where I'm going?
  • What does my presence communicate before I speak?
  • Where am I conforming vs. expressing authentically?

Authentic Alignment

  • You don't have to assimilate to succeed
  • Cultural expression can be professional
  • Consistency builds recognition
  • Dress for the impact you want to make
  • Comfort enables confidence
Reflection

Executive presence isn't about being someone else. It's about being more intentionally, visibly, and powerfully yourself.

Module 2
In Practice

Commanding the Room

Whether it's a meeting, a presentation, or a high-stakes conversation—presence is felt before you speak a word.

Before You Speak

Enter with intention. Make eye contact. Claim your space. Breathe. Let silence work for you.

When You Speak

Lead with your point. Use "I" statements. Pause after key points. Don't apologize for contributing.

Under Pressure

Slow down—don't speed up. Acknowledge the tension. Respond, don't react. Stay grounded in your body.

When Challenged

Thank them for the pushback. Ask clarifying questions. Hold your ground without getting defensive.

The Feedback Trap

Decoding Biased Feedback

Not all feedback about your presence is valid. Learn to distinguish between useful insight and coded bias.

"You're Intimidating"

Translation: Your competence makes someone uncomfortable.
Response: "Can you give me a specific example so I understand the context?"

"You're Too Aggressive"

Translation: You advocated for yourself/your ideas.
Response: "I want to be effective. What specifically would you suggest I do differently?"

"You Need More Gravitas"

Translation: You don't match their mental model of a leader.
Response: "Can you describe what gravitas looks like in our context?"

"You Should Smile More"

Translation: Your competence without pleasantness is disorienting.
Response: "I appreciate the feedback. I focus my energy on delivering results."

Finding Your Way

Adaptation vs. Assimilation

There's a difference between strategic adaptation and losing yourself. The goal is intentional choice, not survival mode.

Strategic Adaptation

  • Choosing when to dial up or down
  • Intentional based on goals
  • You're in control
  • Adds to your repertoire
  • Feels like a choice

Exhausting Assimilation

  • Constant self-monitoring
  • Fear-based, reactive
  • Others are in control
  • Erases parts of you
  • Feels like survival

The question to ask: "Am I making this choice because it serves my goals, or because I'm afraid of judgment?"

Communication Mastery

Language That Commands

Small shifts in your word choice can dramatically change how you're perceived. Here's what to practice.

Instead of "I think maybe..."

Say: "My recommendation is..." or "Based on my analysis..."

Instead of "Does that make sense?"

Say: "What questions do you have?" or "Let me know what resonates."

Instead of "Sorry to interrupt..."

Say: "I'd like to add..." or "Building on that..."

Instead of "This might be a dumb question..."

Say: "I'm curious about..." or simply ask the question.

Module 2
Physical Presence

Your Body Speaks First

55% of communication is non-verbal. Before you say a word, your body has already told a story.

Posture

Stand and sit tall. Shoulders back, feet planted. Take up the space you deserve. Resist shrinking.

Eye Contact

Hold eye contact for 3-5 seconds at a time. In groups, connect with individuals, not the room.

Gestures

Use purposeful hand gestures. Keep them at waist level or above. Avoid self-soothing movements.

Movement

Move with intention. Pause before transitioning. Stillness is more powerful than fidgeting.

Facial Expression

Your face should match your message. Neutral isn't cold—it's composed. Smile when it's genuine.

Voice

Speak from your diaphragm. Vary your pace. Use the pause. End statements downward, not up.

Reflection

Your presence is your first message. Make sure it's saying what you want it to say.
