Foundation

The Forms of Bias

Bias shows up in different ways. Understanding the type helps you choose the right response.

Microaggressions

Subtle, often unintentional slights that communicate hostility or derogatory attitudes. Death by a thousand cuts.

Stereotyping

Assumptions about your abilities, interests, or role based on race, gender, or other identities rather than your actual work.

Systemic Barriers

Policies, practices, or cultural norms that disadvantage certain groups—often invisible to those who benefit from them.

Common Microaggressions
WOC Leaders Face

"You're so articulate!"

Subtext: I'm surprised someone like you speaks well. Impact: Othering, low expectations.

"Where are you really from?"

Subtext: You don't belong here. Impact: Perpetual foreigner status.

Mistaken for support staff

Subtext: You can't possibly be in charge. Impact: Invisibility, having to prove role.

"You're a credit to your race"

Subtext: You're an exception, not the rule. Impact: Isolation from community.

Being talked over or ignored

Subtext: Your voice doesn't matter. Impact: Silencing, erasure of contributions.

Having ideas credited to others

Subtext: You couldn't have thought of that. Impact: Stolen opportunities, invisibility.

Systemic Barriers to Watch For

These aren't personal—they're structural. But they affect you personally.

What It Looks Like

  • Lack of WOC in senior leadership
  • Unequal access to sponsors/mentors
  • Promotion criteria that favor majority styles
  • "Culture fit" that really means conformity
  • Invisible labor assigned to WOC

How to Navigate

  • Build relationships outside the gatekeepers
  • Document your contributions meticulously
  • Seek sponsors who have power and will use it
  • Connect with WOC at peer and senior levels
  • Set boundaries on emotional labor requests
Reflection

The goal isn't to never experience bias. It's to navigate it without losing yourself or your power.

Framework

The PACE Response Framework

Not every situation requires the same response. PACE helps you choose strategically.

P
Pause

Take a breath. Don't react immediately. Buy yourself time to think.

A
Assess

What happened? Who's involved? What's at stake? What outcome do you want?

C
Choose

Select your response: Address now, address later, let go, escalate.

E
Execute

Deliver your response clearly and strategically. Document if needed.

Module 4
Strategy

Your Response Options

You always have choices. No option is weakness—it's strategy.

Address in the Moment

"Can you say more about what you mean by that?" or "I'd like to add my perspective since that was originally my idea." Direct, immediate, clarifying.

Address Later Privately

Pull the person aside afterward. "I wanted to follow up on what you said in the meeting..." Less confrontational, still impactful.

Let It Go (Strategically)

Sometimes the cost isn't worth the battle. This isn't weakness—it's choosing your fights wisely. Document and move on.

Escalate

When the behavior is severe, repeated, or dangerous, involve HR, leadership, or formal channels. Document everything.

Module 4
Practical Scripts

What to Say

Having pre-prepared language makes it easier to respond in the moment.

For Microaggressions

  • "What do you mean by that?"
  • "Can you help me understand?"
  • "That landed differently than you probably intended."
  • "I'm sure you didn't mean it this way, but..."

For Idea Theft

  • "Thanks for building on what I said earlier."
  • "I'm glad you're supporting my recommendation."
  • "To clarify, I first raised this point when..."
  • "I'd like to expand on my original idea..."

Self-Care

The Energy Tax Is Real

Navigating bias takes energy—mental, emotional, physical. You need strategies to replenish.

Emotional Tax

The constant vigilance, self-monitoring, and managing others' discomfort that WOC carry daily.

Cognitive Load

The mental energy spent deciding when to speak up, how to respond, and what battles to fight.

Physical Impact

Chronic stress from navigating bias contributes to real health consequences. Rest is resistance.

Protection

Protecting Your Energy

You can't pour from an empty cup. Build practices that sustain you.

Daily Practices

  • Morning intention setting
  • Micro-breaks to decompress
  • Movement and breathwork

  • Screen boundaries

Community & Support

  • Connect with WOC peers regularly
  • Find your "people who get it"

  • Mentors who've navigated similar
  • Spaces where you're the norm

Module 4
Strategic Relationships

Leveraging Allies

You shouldn't have to fight every battle alone. Real allies take action, not just intentions.

What Real Allies Do

  • Speak up when you're not in the room
  • Credit your ideas publicly
  • Use their privilege to open doors
  • Educate themselves—don't ask you to
  • Accept feedback without defensiveness

How to Cultivate Allies

  • Identify who has power and influence
  • Build genuine relationships first
  • Be specific about what support looks like
  • Give feedback when allyship falls short
  • Recognize and appreciate their efforts

Module 4
Hard Truth

When the Environment
Won't Change

Sometimes the most strategic move is to leave. Knowing when is wisdom, not weakness.

Signs It May Be Time

Persistent patterns despite feedback. Health impacts. No WOC in senior roles. Leadership tolerates bias. You've lost your spark.

Before You Go

Document everything. Build your brand externally. Line up references. Leave on your terms. Don't burn bridges you might need.

Remember: Leaving a toxic environment isn't giving up—it's choosing yourself. Your career is a long game, and some environments aren't worth the cost.
