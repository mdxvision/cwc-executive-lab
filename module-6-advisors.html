How to Earn a Sponsor

Sponsorship isn't asked for‚Äîit's earned. Sponsors invest in people who make them look good and who deliver results.

1

Deliver Exceptional Work

Sponsors stake their reputation on you. Give them undeniable evidence of your excellence. Go beyond expectations on high-visibility projects.

2

Make Your Work Visible

Potential sponsors can't advocate for what they don't know about. Share wins strategically. Present in meetings where leaders attend.

3

Build the Relationship First

Create genuine connection before you need anything. Offer value‚Äîinsights, support on their initiatives, thoughtful perspective.

4

Be Explicit About Your Goals

Once trust is established, clearly share your aspirations. "I'm working toward VP. I'd value your perspective on what I need to get there."

Making the Outreach

Reaching out to people you admire or want to learn from doesn't have to be awkward. Here's a framework:

1

Start with Genuine Appreciation

Reference something specific about their work that resonated with you. Be authentic‚Äîpeople can tell when praise is performative.

2

Establish Relevance

Briefly explain who you are and why you're reaching out. Create a connection point between your work/goals and theirs.

3

Make a Specific, Small Ask

Don't ask for mentorship outright. Ask for one conversation, one question, one piece of advice. Make it easy to say yes.

4

Make It About Them

Show you value their time. Offer flexibility. Express that you're open to whatever they can share.

Sample Outreach Messages

To a Potential Mentor (LinkedIn)

"Hi [Name], I've followed your career at [Company] and was particularly inspired by [specific project/article/talk]. As a [your role] looking to grow into [aspiration], I'd love to learn from your experience navigating [specific challenge]. Would you be open to a 20-minute call? I'd be grateful for any insights you'd be willing to share."

To an Internal Leader

"Hi [Name], I'm [Your name] from [Team]. I've admired how you've [specific observation] and would love to learn more about your approach to [topic]. Would you have 15 minutes for coffee sometime? I'm working on developing [skill/area] and would value your perspective."

Following Up After an Event

"Hi [Name], It was great meeting you at [Event]. Our conversation about [topic] really stuck with me, especially [specific point]. I'd love to continue the conversation‚Äîwould you be open to grabbing coffee or a quick call?"

Nurturing Your Network

Relationships require maintenance. The time to build your network is before you need it‚Äînot when you're job searching or need a favor.

Regular Touch Points

  • Share articles relevant to their interests
  • Congratulate them on wins and milestones
  • Provide updates on how their advice helped
  • Introduce them to valuable connections
  • Invite them to events they'd enjoy

Cadence Guidelines

  • Close advisors: Monthly check-in
  • Key relationships: Quarterly contact
  • Broader network: Bi-annual touchpoint
  • All: React to their content on social

The Give-First Mindset

Always look for ways to add value before asking for help. Generosity builds lasting relationships.

Building Relationships Across Difference

Your board should include people who don't look like you. Here's how to build authentic cross-cultural relationships:

What to Look For

Not everyone will be a true ally. Seek people who:

  • Have a track record of supporting diverse talent
  • Speak up in meetings about inclusion
  • Ask questions and listen more than they advise
  • Acknowledge their own learning journey
  • Take action, not just express intentions

How to Build Trust

Start small and observe:

  • Begin with professional topics
  • Watch how they handle challenging moments
  • Share more as trust develops
  • Let them earn the right to discuss your experience
  • Trust actions over words
