Types of Difficult Conversations

Performance Conversations

  • Addressing underperformance
  • Giving critical feedback
  • Discussing development gaps
  • Communicating difficult decisions

Conflict Conversations

  • Disagreements with peers or leaders
  • Mediating team conflicts
  • Addressing behavior issues
  • Navigating political dynamics

Advocacy Conversations

  • Asking for a raise or promotion
  • Pushing back on unfair treatment
  • Negotiating resources or scope
  • Setting boundaries

High-Stakes Conversations

  • Delivering bad news to stakeholders
  • Termination conversations
  • Crisis communications
  • Addressing bias or discrimination

Preparing for the Conversation

What happens before you speak

The PREP Framework

Before Any Difficult Conversation

P

Purpose

What outcome do you want?

R

Reality

What are the facts?

E

Empathy

What's their perspective?

P

Plan

What will you say/ask?

Questions to Ask Yourself

  • What's my goal for this conversation?
  • What facts do I have (vs. assumptions)?
  • How might they be feeling right now?
  • What's the best possible outcome?
  • What's the worst case and how do I handle it?

Red Flag Check

Don't have the conversation if:

  • You're emotionally triggered
  • You don't have the facts
  • You haven't considered their view
  • You're not clear on your goal
  • You just want to vent or be right

Opening the Conversation

How you start sets the tone for everything. Lead with clarity and respect. Don't soften so much that the message is lost.

Strong Opening Elements

1

State Your Intent

"I want to talk about X because I value our working relationship and want to address this directly."

2

Share Observations

"I've noticed [specific behavior/situation]..." Lead with facts, not judgments.

3

Invite Their View

"I'd like to understand your perspective. Help me understand what's happening from your side."

Example Opening

"I wanted to talk with you directly about the project timeline. I've noticed we've missed two deadlines, and I want to understand what's happening so we can figure out a path forward together."

Avoid

  • "Don't take this personally, but..."
  • Long preambles and softening
  • Starting with accusations
  • "Some people have said..."

Return to Purpose

When derailed, ask: "What am I trying to accomplish?" Redirect yourself back to your goal.

If You Need to Reset

"I want to take a moment to make sure I'm responding thoughtfully. Can we pause for a minute?"

Listening to Understand

In difficult conversations, most people listen to respond, not to understand. Being truly heard often de-escalates tension more than any argument.

Active Listening Techniques

  • Reflect: "What I hear you saying is..."
  • Clarify: "Help me understand..."
  • Validate: "That makes sense given..."
  • Summarize: "So the key points are..."
  • Ask: "What else is important here?"

What Not to Do

  • Interrupt to make your point
  • Prepare your response while they talk
  • Dismiss or minimize their feelings
  • Jump to solutions too quickly
  • Make it about being right

When They Get Defensive

Defensiveness is natural. Don't match it. Here's how to respond:

If They Deny

"I understand you see it differently. Help me understand your perspective on what happened with [specific situation]."

If They Deflect

"I hear that there are other factors. Right now I want to focus on what's in your control. What can you do differently?"

If They Attack

"I can see you're frustrated. I'm not here to attack you‚ÄîI want to find a solution together. Can we reset?"

Stay Grounded

Your calm is contagious. When they escalate:

  • Slow your speech
  • Lower your volume
  • Keep your body open
  • Maintain eye contact
  • Breathe deeply

"What you resist, persists. What you accept, you can address."

Scripts for common difficult conversations

Addressing Performance Issues

1

Be Direct About the Gap

"The expectation is X. What I'm seeing is Y. There's a gap we need to address."

2

Use Specific Examples

"For example, on the Smith project, the deliverable was late by two weeks and had three errors that the client caught."

3

Ask for Their Input

"What's your perspective? What's getting in the way? What do you need to be successful?"

4

Agree on Next Steps

"What I need to see going forward is X. Let's check in weekly. What support do you need from me?"

Remember

Be kind, but be clear. Unclear feedback is unkind‚Äîit robs them of the opportunity to improve.

Asking for What You Deserve

Whether it's a raise, promotion, better assignment, or more resources‚Äîasking is a skill. Don't wait to be noticed. Make your case.

The STAR-A Formula

  • Situation: Context of your contribution
  • Task: What you were responsible for
  • Action: What you specifically did
  • Result: Measurable impact
  • Ask: Your specific request

Example Script

"Over the past year, I've led three major initiatives that delivered $2M in savings. I've consistently exceeded expectations and taken on senior-level responsibilities. Based on my contributions and market data, I'm requesting a promotion to Senior Manager and a compensation adjustment to $X. I'd like to discuss what's possible."

Pushing Back Respectfully

Disagreeing with your boss or a senior leader requires tact but is essential for your credibility:

Disagree with the Approach

"I understand the goal. I have a different perspective on how to get there. Can I share some concerns and an alternative approach?"

Challenge Unrealistic Expectations

"I want to set us up for success. With current resources, I can deliver X by the deadline or Y with more time. Which is the priority?"

Address Unfair Treatment

"I've noticed that I wasn't included in [situation]. I want to understand the decision and share why my involvement would benefit the outcome."

The Formula

Respectful pushback follows this pattern:

  1. Acknowledge their position
  2. Share your concern/perspective
  3. Offer an alternative or question
  4. Seek alignment on next steps

You can disagree without being disagreeable.

Closing Strong

Ending with clarity and commitment

Ending the Conversation

1

Summarize Key Points

"Let me make sure we're aligned. We agreed that [summary of discussion and decisions]."

2

Clarify Next Steps

"The next steps are: you will [action] by [date], and I will [action] by [date]. Does that match your understanding?"

3

Set Follow-Up

"Let's check in on [date] to review progress. I'll send a calendar invite."

4

End on a Constructive Note

"I appreciate you having this conversation with me. I'm confident we can move forward positively."

Document It

After significant conversations, send a brief follow-up email summarizing what was discussed and agreed. This creates a record and ensures alignment.

When Conversations Don't Go Well

Not every difficult conversation ends well. Sometimes people aren't ready, emotions run too high, or agreement isn't possible. Know your options.

Pause and Resume

"I can see we're not in a place to resolve this right now. Let's take a break and come back to this tomorrow with fresh perspective."

Taking a break isn't failure‚Äîit's strategy.

Agree to Disagree

"We see this differently, and that's okay. What I need us to agree on is [specific behavior/action] going forward."

You don't need agreement on everything‚Äîjust on actions.

Escalate When Needed

"I've tried to resolve this directly, and we're not making progress. I think we need to bring [manager/HR] into this conversation."

Escalation isn't tattling‚Äîit's problem-solving.

Know When to Walk Away

Some situations can't be fixed with conversation. Sometimes the answer is to change teams, roles, or organizations.

Not every environment deserves your energy.
